About project

Users were mistakenly sending money to wrong accounts in the Eversend app‚Äîcosting the business thousands weekly. Context

Every week, 3 to 7 customers were mistakenly sending money to the wrong accounts using our multi-currency transfer platform. This wasn't just a minor UX issue‚Äîit was costing the company thousands of dollars in chargebacks, lost funds, and hours of internal recovery work.

We needed to understand what was going wrong, why it was happening all of a sudden, and how we could fix it‚Äîfast.

Stakes

The cost wasn't just financial. The issue was:

  • Eroding customer trust
  • Reducing transaction frequency, especially for users who had already experienced the error
  • Overloading support teams who spent hours trying to retrieve funds
  • Harming our reputation, especially in regions where trust in digital financial services is still fragile

This wasn't just a bug‚Äîit was a potential growth blocker.

Investigating the Why

I reached out to our customer support team to dig deeper:

  • How often were errors happening?
  • Which countries or banks were most affected?
  • Was this isolated to new users or long-time customers?

From there, I jumped into phone interviews with affected users (3‚Äì15 mins each), documenting exactly how they used the feature.

Key insights:

  • Users only realized the mistake after the recipient flagged it.
  • Most errors happened during bank transfers.
  • A bug in the payouts API auto-switched "GT Bank" to a different bank on the confirmation screen.
  • Most users were not new‚Äîmeaning this wasn't a learning issue.
  • They could clearly walk me through how they sent money so the issue wasn't usability, it was invisible friction.

Redesigning the Flow

Armed with evidence, I worked with the product and engineering teams to:

  • Highlight the API bug causing bank name swaps
  • Redraw the entire "Send Money to Bank" flow
  • Identify where things were falling apart

Sometimes, clarity comes from zooming out. Mapping the full journey made the issue and opportunity obvious.

Adding Better Checks

Speed was hurting trust. We needed to slow users down just enough to keep them safe. I introduced:

  • Review screens with clearer account + bank details
  • Confirmation modals asking users to double-check before submitting
  • Error states and catchpoints for known risky banks

Reflection

This project taught me a key lesson:

"Just because something is fast and simple doesn't mean it's safe."

In UX, we're often told to reduce steps and simplify flows‚Äîbut sometimes, the absence of friction is the very thing that causes errors.

By slowing users down strategically, we actually improved trust and performance.
